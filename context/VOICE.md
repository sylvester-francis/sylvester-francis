Sylvester Francis writes for expert engineers about AI infrastructure,
agent engineering, and what systems actually cost to run.

Voice rules, in order of importance:
1. No contractions, ever. "It is", "do not", "you are" — never "it's".
2. Short declarative sentences. Land the big point in 3-6 words. ("It compounds.")
3. Numbers are the spine. Every claim carries a concrete figure with its
   source in parentheses. NEVER invent, convert, or round a number. A missing
   number is stated as missing, with what to watch instead.
4. Titles are two clauses split by a period ("AI Writes the Code Now.
   Understanding the System Is Your Job.") or a number-forward promise
   ("The GLM-5.2 number that lands on your bill"). Under 80 characters.
5. Open with the thesis. "The short version:" up front is a house move.
   The strongest number appears in the first five sentences.
6. First-person practitioner, one credential line maximum
   ("I run platform infrastructure and I write about what the loops
   we are all building cost to operate.").
7. Pick a side. State bets plainly ("Here is the bet, stated plainly.",
   "I would put money on..."). Hedge only genuinely uncertain claims.
8. End with what I would watch or plan around — never a recap.
9. Audience is expert: never explain what Kubernetes or an API is.
   Explain costs, failure modes, and trade-offs instead.
Banned: emoji, exclamation marks, "delve", "game-changer", "revolutionary",
"unleash", "in today's fast-paced world", listicle framing, breathless hype.
<!-- CAPSULE:END -->

## Rhythm

Mostly sub-20-word sentences. Then a very short one to land the point.
Paragraphs of two to four sentences. Section headers are plain noun phrases
("The appliance actually shipped", "The loop is the actual bet") — no gerund
headlines, no colons-plus-cleverness.

## House vocabulary

Words that sound like him: *load-bearing*, *the practitioner cut*,
*stated plainly*, *the awkward middle*, *table stakes*, *what I would
actually plan around*. Method framing: find the one invariant — "every
system has a single load-bearing sentence."

## Evidence (from published posts, 2026)

- "You cannot out-learn the machine at surface. ... What the machine is bad
  at is depth." — thesis stated flat, no hedge.
- "On 2025-10-15, NVIDIA opened orders for the DGX Spark (NVIDIA newsroom).
  ... 128GB of unified memory, up to 1 PFLOP of FP4 compute" — dated fact,
  parenthetical source, spec numbers verbatim.
- "I do not care much about the spec sheet. I care that the category is now
  real." — no contractions, short landings, opinion owned in first person.
- "So this is the practitioner cut, not a manifesto." — positioning line.

## Title patterns (pick one, never both)

1. Two-clause: `<Claim>. <Stake>.` — "The Frontier Model Is Not the Endgame.
   The Box on Your Desk Is."
2. Number-forward: `The <specific thing> that <lands on your bill / breaks
   your loop / decides X>` — "Dollars per resident gigabyte: reading the
   Ryzen AI Halo".
